Infographic: Retro Video Game Popularity

Problem Statement:
As an introduction to Adobe Illustrator, design an infographic of a current issue and model it after another similar infographic.

Plan:
Choosing one of the most important topics on earth, the rising cost of retro video games, present a similarly themed informative design.

Team:
Patrick Harper-Joles

Technology:
Illustrator

Mildly Interesting:
During this project I gained a very close relationship to all of the retro video game resale shops in the Seattle/Bellevue, WA area. I made many calls and more visits in person to ask detailed research questions about what they thought was driving up prices in the market. The greater Seattle area has one of the best resale communities in the nation!

  • New, Used, or Collectible?

    Here I am focusing on the elements of; item recognition (a large list of highly successful games), highlighting current resale price, showing the beginning of resale price tracking (collected statistics dated 2008 and after).

    Through research and interviews into the retro video game industry it became clear to me that not all games increase over time. Two items seem to play into a game's eventual rising cost, community status and production run. Community status plays into whether a game has value amongst the community, usually denoted as being 'good' games or not. But it is not always that simple, sometimes a game can have status simply because it is bad, maybe comically so. Additionally, status can rise if something with the game happens socially, such as adoption as a new speed running game for instance can cause a spike in demand. Production run, speaks to the number of physical copies of the game produced by the publisher. Some small publishers produce drastically smaller print runs than larger publishers.

  • Colored for Collection

    Here I am focusing on the elements of; image recognition (N64 cartridge shape), similar color usage (leveraging my color base for known specials, Zelda = Gold), and highlighting small numbers of a larger whole.

    Before purposefully built 'special edition' video games became a mainstay in the video game industry, they were more of a differentiation tactic than a collectors item. When the Sony Playstation released in 1995, they moved away from traditional cartridge based video games and began to utilize CD technology to press the games onto discs, drastically decreasing production costs. Nintendo's answer to the Playstation, the Nintendo 64, continued to use cartridges despite the cost detriment to 3rd party vendors. However, this allowed publishers to explore different colored cartridges to make their games stand out, something that a disc had trouble doing.

  • Cost timeline mapped

    Here I am focusing on the elements of; item recognition (Super Metroid is a very popular game in most communities), highlighting starting MSRP and current resale price, from the research I had gathered I also attempted to explain the largest jump with highlighted text.

    Despite being readily available for many years after release, most video games lost value for a long time. With a revival of pop culture and nostalgia in the early to mid 2000's caused many new video game collectors and new gamers interested in games that they never had the chance to buy new at retail, entered the used market. Though many factors appear to have shaped the slow increase of prices over time, there does seem to be a bit of a formula to a game releasing at retail, losing value over time, then beginning to rebound over time as traded supply of the game dwindles.
